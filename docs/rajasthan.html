Rajasthan

← All destinations

Rajasthan, a state in northwestern India, is noted for its vibrant and diverse cultural history.

Language & culture

The predominant language is Hindi, but many areas also speak Rajasthani and its dialects, including Marwari, Mewari, and Dhundhari. Folk music is diverse, with varieties such as Ghoomar, Kalbelia, Terahtali, and Mhari Ghoomar. Rajasthani literature has a lengthy history of poetry and folklore — famous poets from the region include Surdas and Meera Bai. The state is known for traditional art forms like miniature paintings, block printing, and embroidered fabrics such as zardozi and bandhej (tie-dye).

Cuisine

Rajasthani cuisine is rich, savory, and frequently spicy. Some key dishes:

  • Dal Baati Churma — a classic of lentils (dal), wheat-flour dumplings (baati), and a dessert of crushed wheat and ghee (churma).
  • Gatte ki Sabzi — a spicy curry with gram-flour dumplings.
  • Laal Maas — a spicy mutton curry made with red chiles.
  • Ker Sangri — a unique dish made with desert beans and berries.

Architecture

Rajasthan's architecture combines Hindu, Mughal, and Rajput traditions, with spectacular forts, palaces, temples, and havelis.

  • Amber Fort and Jaipur City Palace in Jaipur.
  • Udaipur's City Palace and Lake Palace.
  • Mehrangarh Fort and Umaid Bhawan Palace in Jodhpur.
  • Jaisalmer Fort — the "Golden Fort," among the world's largest living forts.

Dress & faith

Traditional dress is vibrant and reflects the state's royal heritage. Women often wear ghagras (long skirts) with blouses and odhni (veils); rural women may wear colorful sarees, while urban women wear modern variants. Men typically wear dhotis, kurta-pajamas, or bandis (vests) with a safa (turban) for formal occasions. Rajasthan is also a land of diverse religious beliefs, home to the famed Brahma Temple in Pushkar, Jain temples in Mount Abu, and Vaishnavism traditions.
